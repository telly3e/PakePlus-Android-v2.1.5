Question 2 (a): Ethernet CSMA/CD#

Given Data:

  • Bandwidth: 10 Mbps (not needed for bit-time calculations)
  • Propagation Delay (\(T_{prop}\)): 225 bit times
  • Jam Signal: 48 bit times
  • Back-off slot time: 512 bit times
  • Minimum frame size: 512 bit times (implied for standard Ethernet)
  • Back-off values: \(K_A = 0\), \(K_B = 1\)
  • Start time: \(t=0\) for both A and B.

Timeline Analysis:

  1. Collision: A and B start at \(t=0\). They are at opposite ends, so the signals cross in the middle, but the collision is detected only when the signal from the other node arrives.
    • Time collision is detected by A and B = \(T_{prop} = 225\) bit times.
  2. Jamming: Immediately upon detection (\(t=225\)), both nodes send a Jam signal.
    • Jamming ends at \(t = 225 + 48 = 273\) bit times.
  3. Back-off: After the jam signal, nodes wait for their back-off interval (\(K \times 512\)).

i) At what time A begins retransmission?

A's back-off value \(K=0\).

\[Wait\_Time_A = 0 \times 512 = 0 \text{ bit times}\]

A is ready to retransmit immediately after the Jam signal is finished.

\[Time_{start\_A} = \text{Time}_{detect} + \text{Jam}_{duration} + \text{Wait}_{A}\]\[Time_{start\_A} = 225 + 48 + 0 = \mathbf{273 \text{ bit times}}\]

(Note: In a real physical scenario, A would also perform carrier sensing. However, in standard algorithmic exam problems of this type, the transmission time is calculated as the moment the back-off timer expires.)

ii) At what time A’s signal reach B?

It takes \(T_{prop}\) (225 bit times) for the signal to travel from A to B.

\[Time_{arrival\_at\_B} = Time_{start\_A} + T_{prop}\]\[Time_{arrival\_at\_B} = 273 + 225 = \mathbf{498 \text{ bit times}}\]

iii) Does B refrain from retransmission at its scheduled time?

  • B's Scheduled Time: B has \(K=1\). It waits \(1 \times 512 = 512\) bit times after jamming.

    \[Time_{schedule\_B} = 273 (\text{Jam end}) + 512 (\text{Backoff}) = \mathbf{785 \text{ bit times}}\]
  • Channel Status at B:
    • A's signal arrives at B at \(t = 498\) (from part ii).
    • A is sending a minimum size frame (512 bits).
    • A's frame occupies B's receiver from \(t=498\) to \(t=498 + 512 = 1010\).
  • Conclusion: When B wakes up at \(t=785\) to transmit, it senses the channel is busy (occupied by A's incoming frame).
  • Answer: Yes, B refrains from retransmitting because it senses the carrier (A's signal) on the line.

iv) Assuming no other nodes are active, will the retransmission of A and B be successful?

  • A: A transmitted at 273. At that time, B was in its wait state (silent). B detected A's signal at 498 and deferred. Therefore, A's transmission completes without collision.
  • B: Once A finishes, the channel becomes idle. B will sense the idle channel and transmit its frame. Since no other nodes are active, B will also succeed.
  • Answer: Yes, both will be successful. (A succeeds first, followed by B).

Question 2 (c): Differences between IEEE 802.3 and IEEE 802.11#

Here are three key differences between wired (802.3 Ethernet) and wireless (802.11 Wi-Fi) LANs:

  1. Media Access Control (CSMA/CD vs CSMA/CA):
    • IEEE 802.3 uses CSMA/CD (Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Detection). Stations listen to the wire while transmitting. If they detect a collision (voltage spike), they stop immediately.
    • IEEE 802.11 uses CSMA/CA (Collision Avoidance). Wireless radios generally cannot transmit and receive on the same frequency simultaneously (half-duplex), so they cannot detect collisions while sending. Instead, they use acknowledgement packets (ACKs) and mechanisms like RTS/CTS (Request to Send / Clear to Send) to avoid collisions before they happen.
  2. Error Control and Reliability:
    • IEEE 802.3 assumes a high-quality, low-error physical medium (cables). It typically does not use link-layer acknowledgments (ACKs) for data frames; it relies on upper layers (like TCP) for error recovery.
    • IEEE 802.11 operates in a noisy environment subject to interference. It uses Stop-and-Wait ARQ (Automatic Repeat Request), requiring an ACK for every unicast frame to ensure it was received correctly. If no ACK is received, the frame is retransmitted at the link layer.
  3. Physical Environment and Interference:
    • IEEE 802.3 uses guided media (copper or fiber). It is less susceptible to interference and signal boundaries are well-defined (the physical cable).
    • IEEE 802.11 uses unguided media (radio waves). It is subject to attenuation, multipath propagation, and interference from other devices (microwaves, cordless phones). It also suffers from the "Hidden Node" and "Exposed Node" problems, which do not exist in wired switching.

Q3 - Network Throughput Calculation for Buffer Management#

Solution#

Problem Objective#

To prevent the buffers at node B from flooding, the rate at which data arrives at node B (from A) must be less than or equal to the rate at which data leaves node B (towards C).

\[Throughput_{A \to B} \leq Throughput_{B \to C}\]

Step 1: Analyze Link A \(\to\) B (Input Rate)#

First, we calculate the effective throughput of the link from A to B using the given parameters.

  • Distance (\(d_{AB}\)): \(4000 \text{ km}\)
  • Bandwidth (\(R_{AB}\)): \(100 \text{ kbps} = 100,000 \text{ bps}\)
  • Frame Size (\(L\)): \(1000 \text{ bits}\)
  • Propagation Speed: \(5 \mu\text{s/km}\)
  • Protocol: Sliding Window (Window Size \(W = 3\))
  1. Calculate Propagation Delay (\(T_{prop}\)):
\[T_{prop} = \text{Distance} \times \text{Propagation Speed}\]\[T_{prop} = 4000 \text{ km} \times 5 \times 10^{-6} \text{ s/km} = 0.02 \text{ s} = 20 \text{ ms}\]
  1. Calculate Transmission Delay (\(T_{trans}\)):
\[T_{trans} = \frac{\text{Frame Size}}{\text{Bandwidth}}\]\[T_{trans} = \frac{1000 \text{ bits}}{100,000 \text{ bps}} = 0.01 \text{ s} = 10 \text{ ms}\]
  1. Determine Effective Throughput:

Since ACK frames are negligible, the total Round Trip Time (RTT) or "cycle time" for one window is:

\[RTT = T_{trans} + 2 \times T_{prop}\]\[RTT = 10 \text{ ms} + 2(20 \text{ ms}) = 50 \text{ ms} = 0.05 \text{ s}\]

We check if the window size (\(W=3\)) is large enough to fill the pipe.

  • Time to send one full window: \(3 \text{ frames} \times 10 \text{ ms/frame} = 30 \text{ ms}\).
  • Since \(30 \text{ ms} < 50 \text{ ms}\) (RTT), the link is not fully utilized. The sender transmits 3 frames and then waits.

Effective Throughput (\(Th_{AB}\)):

\[Th_{AB} = \frac{\text{Data Sent in one Cycle}}{\text{Cycle Time}}\]\[Th_{AB} = \frac{3 \times 1000 \text{ bits}}{0.05 \text{ s}} = \frac{3000}{0.05} = 60,000 \text{ bps} = 60 \text{ kbps}\]

Step 2: Analyze Link B \(\to\) C (Output Rate)#

Now we determine the required rate for the link from B to C. Let the unknown transmission rate be \(R_{BC}\).

  • Distance (\(d_{BC}\)): \(1000 \text{ km}\)
  • Protocol: Stop-and-Wait (equivalent to Window Size \(W = 1\))
  1. Calculate Propagation Delay (\(T_{prop}\)):
\[T_{prop} = 1000 \text{ km} \times 5 \times 10^{-6} \text{ s/km} = 0.005 \text{ s} = 5 \text{ ms}\]
  1. Calculate Transmission Delay (\(T_{trans}\)):
\[T_{trans} = \frac{1000}{R_{BC}}\]
  1. Determine Effective Throughput Formula:

For Stop-and-Wait, the cycle time is \(T_{trans} + 2 \times T_{prop}\).

\[Th_{BC} = \frac{\text{Frame Size}}{T_{trans} + 2 \times T_{prop}}\]\[Th_{BC} = \frac{1000}{\frac{1000}{R_{BC}} + 2(0.005)} = \frac{1000}{\frac{1000}{R_{BC}} + 0.01}\]

Step 3: Equate and Solve#

To avoid buffer flooding, the output rate must equal (or exceed) the input rate:

\[Th_{BC} \geq Th_{AB}\]\[\frac{1000}{\frac{1000}{R_{BC}} + 0.01} = 60,000\]

Solving for \(R_{BC}\):

  1. Divide both sides by 1000:

    \[\frac{1}{\frac{1000}{R_{BC}} + 0.01} = 60\]

  2. Take the reciprocal of both sides:

    \[\frac{1000}{R_{BC}} + 0.01 = \frac{1}{60}\]

  3. Subtract 0.01 (\(1/100\)) from both sides:

    \[\frac{1000}{R_{BC}} = \frac{1}{60} - \frac{1}{100}\]

  4. Find a common denominator (300) for the fractions:

    \[\frac{1000}{R_{BC}} = \frac{5}{300} - \frac{3}{300}\]

     

    \[\frac{1000}{R_{BC}} = \frac{2}{300} = \frac{1}{150}\]

  5. Solve for \(R_{BC}\):

    \[R_{BC} = 1000 \times 150\]

     

    \[R_{BC} = 150,000 \text{ bps}\]

Final Answer#

The minimum transmission rate required between nodes B and C is 150 kbps.

Q4 - TCP Congestion Window Analysis#

i) Explain the term "slow start", indicate the times at which TCP is in slow start.#

Explanation: Slow Start is a phase in TCP congestion control where the congestion window (\(cwnd\)) increases exponentially. During this phase, the \(cwnd\) starts at 1 MSS (Maximum Segment Size) and doubles every Round Trip Time (RTT) as long as acknowledgments are received correctly. This phase continues until the \(cwnd\) reaches the slow-start-threshold (\(ssthresh\)) or a packet loss occurs.

Times: Based on the exponential growth pattern (1, 2, 4, 8...) shown in Figure 3, TCP is in Slow Start during the following time intervals:

  • Time 1 to 4 (\(cwnd\) grows 1 → 2 → 4 → 8)
  • Time 12 to 15 (After the timeout at T12, \(cwnd\) resets to 1 and grows exponentially)
  • Time 24 to 27 (After the timeout at T24, \(cwnd\) resets to 1 and grows exponentially up to the new \(ssthresh\) of 8)
  • Time 34 to 38 (After the timeout at T34, \(cwnd\) grows exponentially again)

ii) Explain the term "congestion avoidance", indicate the times at which TCP is in congestion avoidance.#

Explanation: Congestion Avoidance is the phase that typically follows Slow Start when the \(cwnd\) reaches the \(ssthresh\) value. In this phase, TCP becomes more conservative to avoid congesting the network. The congestion window increases linearly (additive increase), typically growing by 1 MSS per RTT, rather than exponentially.

Times: Based on the linear growth pattern shown in Figure 3, TCP is in Congestion Avoidance during the following intervals:

  • Time 4 to 9 (Linear growth from 8 to 14)
  • Time 15 to 23 (Linear growth starting after the \(cwnd\) hits 8)
  • Time 27 to 33 (Linear growth starting after the \(cwnd\) hits 8)
  • Time 38 to 40 (Linear growth continues at the end of the graph)

iii) Explain the term "fast recovery", indicate the times at which TCP is fast recovery.#

Explanation: Fast Recovery is a state entered when packet loss is detected via 3 duplicate ACKs (rather than a timeout). Instead of dropping the window to 1 (as in Slow Start), TCP performs "Fast Retransmit" and cuts the \(ssthresh\) to half of the current \(cwnd\). The \(cwnd\) is then set to \(ssthresh + 3\) MSS. If subsequent duplicate ACKs are received, the window may inflate further; once a new ACK is received, it exits to Congestion Avoidance.

Times:

  • Time 9 to 10: At Time 9, the window is 14. A loss is detected via triple duplicate ACKs. The system transitions into Fast Recovery. The value at Time 10 is 10, which corresponds to the formula: \(new\_ssthresh (14/2 = 7) + 3 = 10\). The period roughly between detecting the loss at T9 and the subsequent timeout at T12 represents the Fast Recovery attempt (specifically visible at Time 10).

iv) Indicate the times at which packets are lost via timeout.#

Packet loss via timeout is characterized by a drastic drop in the congestion window size to 1 MSS, followed by a Slow Start phase.

Times:

  • Time 12 (\(cwnd\) drops from ~11 to 1)
  • Time 24 (\(cwnd\) drops from ~16 to 1)
  • Time 34 (\(cwnd\) drops from ~19 to 1)

v) Indicate the times at which packets are lost via triple ACK.#

Packet loss via triple duplicate ACK is characterized by the congestion window dropping to roughly half its value (specifically \(ssthresh + 3\) in Reno), followed by Congestion Avoidance or Fast Recovery, rather than resetting to 1.

Times:

  • Time 9 (\(cwnd\) drops from 14 to 10, indicating a cut to half (7) plus 3).
